*One idea, three forms, two platforms abandoned — until it stopped being a luxury and started being survival.*

I funded, designed, and built this myself — concept through Kickstarter through ship. Across its life it was a web service, then a mobile game, then a tabletop game. What stayed constant was the core idea: turning your inner life — what you want, what you're carrying, what you're trying to protect — into something you actively play rather than passively manage. What changed, twice, was the form. The reason it had to change is the whole story.

![Pivots](/pivots.png)

## Form one: "your wishes become quests."

It started as a coaching web service — you'd name what you wanted, and the system turned those wishes into quests to pursue. It worked on paper and didn't in practice: most players didn't return after the first session. I assumed at first I'd built it wrong, and spent a while fixing surfaces before I accepted that wasn't it. Low retention rarely means the thing is broken — more often the reason to come back just isn't strong enough. So I started treating the concept as a hypothesis to test rather than a product to defend.

## Form two: "tend your garden of inner thoughts."

My first real bet was that the core was sound and just needed more pull, so I rebuilt it as a mobile game — the concept as a garden of your inner thoughts that you tend over time. There were signs of life: I showed it at the Daegu Content & Creator Fair in 2025, and one visitor came back to the booth a second time the same day. But the more useful result was what players said around it. The recurring word was *luxury.* Something nice to have. An indulgence.

That word reframed the whole problem — and the metaphor had been telling me the same thing in advance. A garden you tend *is* a luxury: optional, aspirational, the first thing dropped from a busy life. The booth revisit was real pull, but it was the pull of something pleasant, not something needed — and pleasant doesn't retain. I'd gamified the concept but kept its motivation aspirational, and the aspiration was the retention problem all along. Polish doesn't fix "optional"; it just makes a luxury nicer. I'd changed the format and preserved the exact thing that was broken.

## Form three: "reclaim the margin."

So I stopped changing the form and changed the stakes. I kept the core concept and rebuilt it a third time, now as a tactical survival solo TTRPG. You start with almost nothing — three margin slots out of twenty-one — and the whole game is the fight to win the rest back without losing what you've clawed up. That structure runs two motivations at once: the pull of reclaiming what you're missing, and the fear of giving back what you've earned. Aspiration is easy to postpone; a deficit you're actively climbing out of, with every gain now at risk, is not. Survival and loss-aversion create a reason to come back that "this is enjoyable" never will.

![Unfolding Quest: Reclaim the Margin](/unfolding-quest.png)

## What shipped.

The survival version is what I took through Kickstarter and shipped: $1,000 funded and more than 30 copies sold. Modest numbers — and that's the point. They represent the whole loop closed by one person across three platforms: concept, two pivots, funding, and a finished game in players' hands.

![Kickstarter](/kickstarter.png)

The softer signal was booth behavior, and I want to be precise about what it was: not a measurement, just something I noticed across two events a year apart. In 2025 the garden version drew a single visitor back a second time. In 2026 I showed the survival version at PlayX4 and saw several visitors make return trips to the booth. Two different games, two different crowds, so it's anecdotal — but the direction lined up with the bet: the version built to pull people back seemed to.

## What I'd do differently.

I'd test the *motivation* before rebuilding the *form.* I changed platforms twice — web to mobile to tabletop — while carrying the same aspirational framing through each one, and that framing was the real problem the whole time. I was loyal to the right thing (the concept) and slow to question the thing I should have (why anyone comes back). Now I pressure-test the reason for return before I invest in what they return to.
